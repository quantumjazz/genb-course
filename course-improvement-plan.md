# Course Improvement Plan: "Society, Economics and Management"

## Critical Assessment of Current State

### What exists now

The course currently has a syllabus describing 6 lectures and 6 seminars, plus four sets of slides (one full introductory lecture and three topic-specific decks). The syllabus is in Bulgarian, the textbook is Milgrom & Roberts (1992) *Economics, Organization and Management*, and the slides are in Quarto/Reveal.js format.

### Diagnosis: Why is this course "a total disaster"?

Having read all the materials carefully, I see several interconnected problems:

**1. Structural mismatch: 6 lectures cannot cover the textbook's scope**

The syllabus tries to compress 17 chapters of Milgrom & Roberts into just 6 lectures. The result is that each lecture is a laundry list of concepts, names, and case studies rather than a coherent learning experience. Lecture 1 alone lists Douglass North, the Industrial Revolution, Toyota, Hudson's Bay Company, Northwest Company, Salomon Brothers, Eastern European transition, AND three types of entrepreneurship — that is 3-4 lectures worth of material jammed into one session.

**2. The syllabus is a list of keywords, not a learning design**

Each lecture entry is structured as "concepts / facts / big authors" — essentially a Wikipedia-style inventory. There are no learning objectives, no progression logic, no indication of what students should *be able to do* after each session. For a 2nd-year bachelor course in an entrepreneurship program, this is far too abstract and encyclopedic.

**3. Massive gap between the syllabus (6 lectures) and the requirement (16-18 sessions)**

The course needs 16-18 lectures and seminars, but only 6 lectures and 6 seminars are designed. Even counting all 12, there's a shortfall. More importantly, the 6+6 structure doesn't reflect a coherent 16-18 session arc.

**4. Slide quality is uneven**

- The **Module 1 slides** ("Why do firms exist?") are actually quite good — well-structured, with engaging examples, thought experiments, clear narrative arc, and questions for reflection. This is a solid foundation to build on.
- The **three topic slides** (Bounded Rationality, Adverse Selection, Moral Hazard) are much weaker: dense with formal math (utility functions, optimization problems, FOC conditions), no narrative structure, no engaging examples beyond textbook classics, and pitched at a graduate microeconomics level rather than a 2nd-year bachelor course in entrepreneurship.

**5. Audience mismatch**

The formal principal-agent models, incentive compatibility constraints, and optimization problems in slides 2-4 are appropriate for an MSc/PhD economics course. For 2nd-year entrepreneurship students, the course needs to convey the *intuition and business relevance* of these ideas, with math used sparingly and illustratively rather than as the main language.

**6. Missing pedagogical elements**

No assessment structure, no reading assignments tied to specific sessions, no group project or simulation framework, no progressive skill building.

## Recommended Course Architecture: 18 Sessions (12 Lectures + 6 Seminars)

### Design Principles

1. **Module structure**: 6 modules, each consisting of 2 lectures + 1 seminar = 18 sessions total
2. **Narrative arc**: Move from "Why do organizations exist?" through the core problems (information, incentives, coordination) to "How do we design better organizations?"
3. **Appropriate level**: Intuition-first, with selective formal models kept simple (no graduate-level optimization). Use the Module 1 slides as the quality benchmark.
4. **Case-driven**: Every module anchored in real business cases, with preference for contemporary and Bulgarian/regional examples alongside the textbook classics
5. **Active learning**: Seminars feature case discussions, simulations, or structured debates — not passive review

### Proposed Module Plan

#### Module 1: Why Do Organizations Exist? (Sessions 1-3)

**Lecture 1: The Birth of the Modern Business Organization**
- The world before and after the Industrial Revolution
- Institutions and economic growth (North)
- The three faces of entrepreneurship (Baumol)
- *Textbook*: M&R Ch. 1

**Lecture 2: Firms vs. Markets — Transaction Costs**
- Coase's question: Why do firms exist?
- Transaction costs: types and examples
- Firm boundaries: when to make vs. buy
- Efficiency as an organizing principle
- *Textbook*: M&R Ch. 2

**Seminar 1: Case Study Workshop**
- Cases: Toyota Production System, Hudson's Bay vs. Northwest Company, Salomon Brothers
- Students analyze each case through the lens of transaction costs, coordination, and motivation
- Small group discussion + presentation

*Note: The existing Module 1 slides are strong and can serve as the basis for Lectures 1-2 with moderate splitting and refinement.*

---

#### Module 2: Contracts, Rationality, and Their Limits (Sessions 4-6)

**Lecture 3: Perfect and Imperfect Contracts**
- What would a "perfect contract" look like?
- Why contracts are always incomplete: bounded rationality (Simon)
- Renegotiation costs, hold-up problems, specific investments
- *Textbook*: M&R Ch. 5 (first half)

**Lecture 4: When Information Is Private**
- Asymmetric information: who knows what?
- Adverse selection: the Market for Lemons (Akerlof) — intuitive treatment
- Signaling: education as a costly signal (Spence) — intuitive treatment
- Screening and self-selection mechanisms
- *Textbook*: M&R Ch. 5 (second half)

**Seminar 2: The Lemons Game + Signaling Exercise**
- Classroom simulation of a "lemons market" (simplified trading game)
- Discussion: Where do students see adverse selection in real life? (used cars, insurance, hiring, startups seeking funding)
- The De Beers case: information asymmetry and investment in bargaining position

*Note: The existing "Bounded Rationality" and "Adverse Selection" slides contain useful content but need to be completely reworked — strip out the formal optimization, keep the intuitions, examples, and models at an accessible level (e.g., the Akerlof numerical example is good, but the revelation principle and IC constraints should be simplified to verbal/graphical reasoning).*

---

#### Module 3: Moral Hazard — Hidden Actions and Incentives (Sessions 7-9)

**Lecture 5: The Problem of Hidden Action**
- Moral hazard: definition and everyday examples
- Insurance and misbehavior
- Employee shirking, managerial misbehavior
- The US Savings & Loan crisis as a case study
- *Textbook*: M&R Ch. 6

**Lecture 6: Designing Incentives Under Moral Hazard**
- Monitoring vs. incentive contracts
- Risk sharing and incentive intensity (intuitive treatment of the trade-off)
- The four principles of incentive pay (informativeness, intensity, monitoring, equal compensation) — explained with business examples
- *Textbook*: M&R Ch. 7

**Seminar 3: Incentive Design Challenge**
- Case: Gig economy platforms (Uber, Deliveroo) — how do they solve moral hazard?
- Exercise: Students design an incentive contract for a specific scenario (e.g., a startup hiring its first salesperson)
- Discussion: When do incentives backfire?

*Note: The existing "Moral Hazard" slides are too formal. Keep the insurance example and the gig economy case, but replace the optimization framework with intuitive reasoning, payoff tables, and decision trees.*

---

#### Module 4: Ownership, Property Rights, and Rents (Sessions 10-12)

**Lecture 7: Who Owns What and Why It Matters**
- Property rights: residual control and residual returns
- The Coase Theorem reconsidered: when bargaining fails
- Tragedy of the commons
- The ethics of private property
- *Textbook*: M&R Ch. 9

**Lecture 8: Efficiency Wages, Reputation, and Rent-Seeking**
- Efficiency wages: paying above market to motivate (Shapiro-Stiglitz — intuitive version)
- Reputation as a contract enforcement mechanism
- Rent-seeking: productive vs. wasteful competition for rents
- Organizational design to minimize influence costs
- *Textbook*: M&R Ch. 8

**Seminar 4: Property Rights Simulation + Bulgarian Transition Case**
- Labor market auction simulation (as referenced in original syllabus)
- Case discussion: Privatization in Bulgaria — what went right, what went wrong?
- Connect to Baumol's framework: what type of entrepreneurship did the institutional environment encourage?

---

#### Module 5: The Boundaries and Structure of the Firm (Sessions 13-15)

**Lecture 9: Vertical Boundaries — Make or Buy?**
- Vertical integration vs. outsourcing
- Asset specificity and the hold-up problem (Williamson)
- Franchising as a hybrid organizational form
- *Textbook*: M&R Ch. 16 (vertical boundaries section)

**Lecture 10: Horizontal Scope, Alliances, and Networks**
- Horizontal integration, diversification, mergers
- Business alliances and strategic partnerships
- Keiretsu and other inter-firm networks (NUMMI case)
- The modern firm: platforms, ecosystems, globalization
- *Textbook*: M&R Ch. 16 (horizontal section) + Ch. 17

**Seminar 5: Organizational Design Workshop**
- Case: NUMMI (GM-Toyota joint venture) — why did it work?
- Exercise: Students map the boundaries of a real company (e.g., a Bulgarian firm or a well-known platform business) and argue what should be in-house vs. outsourced
- Discussion: The future of the firm in the age of platforms and AI

---

#### Module 6: People, Compensation, and Organizational Change (Sessions 16-18)

**Lecture 11: Employment, Compensation, and Motivation**
- Employment as a relationship, not just a transaction
- Internal labor markets, promotions, and career incentives
- Forms of compensation: piece rates, bonuses, equity, group incentives
- Job design and complementarities
- *Textbook*: M&R Ch. 10-12 (selective)

**Lecture 12: The Evolution of Organizations — Past, Present, Future**
- How organizations evolve: complementarities, inertia, disruption
- The service economy and globalization
- Innovation in organizational form: remote work, DAOs, platform cooperatives
- Synthesis: What have we learned? The recurring themes of coordination, motivation, and institutional context
- *Textbook*: M&R Ch. 17

**Seminar 6: Capstone — Organizational Diagnosis Project Presentations**
- Student teams present their semester-long organizational diagnosis project (see Assessment below)
- Peer feedback and discussion
- Course synthesis and reflection

## Slide Production Plan

### What to keep
- **Module 1 slides** (Lectures 1-2): These are well-crafted and can be split into two lectures with minor additions. Use them as the stylistic and quality template for all other slides.

### What to rework from scratch
- **All other slides** (Lectures 3-12): The existing three decks (Bounded Rationality, Adverse Selection, Moral Hazard) should be treated as content reference only. The actual slides need to be rebuilt at the right level — intuition-first, case-driven, with math used only illustratively and kept to algebra/simple diagrams.

### Slide design principles (following the Module 1 model)
- Clear narrative arc within each lecture (Parts I, II, III...)
- Thought experiments and rhetorical questions to engage students
- Tables for comparisons, not for formulas
- Real-world examples and case studies in every section
- "Questions for reflection" at the end
- Recommended reading tied to specific chapters
- "Key authors" section as cultural literacy, not exhaustive bibliography

## Suggested Assessment Structure

1. **Seminar participation** (20%): Active engagement in case discussions and exercises
2. **Mid-term assignment** (30%): Written analysis of a case study using course concepts (individual, 1500-2000 words)
3. **Organizational diagnosis project** (40%): Team project — students select a real organization (ideally a Bulgarian firm or startup), analyze it through the lens of the course (transaction costs, incentive design, boundaries, property rights), and present recommendations. Delivered as a written report + Seminar 6 presentation.
4. **Reflection essay** (10%): Short personal reflection on the most important insight from the course and how it applies to their entrepreneurial ambitions.

## Summary

The core ideas from Milgrom & Roberts are excellent for this audience — but they need to be **translated** from graduate economics into a language and format appropriate for 2nd-year entrepreneurship students. The existing Module 1 slides prove this is possible and provide a clear template. The main work is: restructure the course into 18 sessions, rebuild the analytical slides without the graduate-level formalism, and add active-learning elements (cases, simulations, a semester project) that make the ideas stick.
